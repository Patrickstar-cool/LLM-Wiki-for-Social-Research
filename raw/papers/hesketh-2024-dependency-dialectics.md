Chris Hesketh (2024). "Towards a new dialectics of dependency theory." Alternautas. Open access (CC BY-NC-SA 4.0).

Chris Hesketh is an Assistant Professor in International Relations at the University of Sussex. He has an inter-disciplinary research agenda combining political economy, geography, political theory, and Latin American studies.

## Core Argument

Dependency theory is arguably the most important theoretical tradition to emerge from Latin America. Functioning as a vital source of counter-representation and contestation, it challenged the prevailing developmental orthodoxies of the time. During its heyday in the 1960s and early 1970s it served simultaneously to critique prevailing power relations within the global political economy and as a political programme for domestic and regional transformation, defined in terms of self-determination and political autonomy.

However, with the broader crisis of developmentalism in the 1970s, a counter-movement to the radicalism of dependency analysis emerged: authoritarian populism. The victory of authoritarian populism helped contribute to the death of Third Worldism as a political project and with it, dependency theory fell into decline.

## The Call for Renewal

In the current conjuncture, this paper calls for a new dialectics of dependency theory. A reinvigorated dependency critique is needed to address:
1. The prevailing developmentalism of the left in Latin America that has remained in thrall to extractivism and thus continues the region's peripheral role as a commodity exporter to the Global North, built on cheap nature and cheap labour.
2. Contemporary modes of authoritarian populism and statism being celebrated geopolitically through the BRICS grouping.

## Innovation: Spatial Turn

Hesketh makes the case for considering dependency theory as a radical contribution to the literature on the production of space. A renewed dependency theory can thus:
- Challenge the state-centrism present in its original formulation
- Address its lack of ecological concern
- Engage with neo-extractivism and the "planetary mine" (Arboleda 2020)

## Key Theoretical Engagements

- Uneven and combined development (Brenner, Bieler & Morton)
- Extractivism and neo-extractivism (Acosta, Gudynas, Burchardt & Dietz)
- Spatial political economy (Avilés 2024, Hesketh 2024a)
- Indigenous resistance at the frontiers of accumulation (Hesketh 2025)
- Dependency and intersectionality: gender, race (Antunes de Oliveira 2021)

## Significance

This paper represents a major contemporary effort to revitalize dependency theory by:
1. Integrating spatial theory and political ecology
2. Addressing the ecological blind spot of classical dependency theory
3. Connecting dependency analysis to contemporary forms of authoritarian populism and extractivism
