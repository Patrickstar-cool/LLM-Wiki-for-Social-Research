# Qualitative Research in an Era of AI: A Pragmatic Approach to Data Analysis, Workflow, and Computation

**Authors:** Corey M. Abramson (Rice University), Tara Prendergast (University of Arizona), Zhuofan Li (Virginia Tech), Daniel Dohan (UC San Francisco)

**Publication:** FORTHCOMING in Annual Review of Sociology, Volume 52 (2026)

**License:** CC BY-NC-ND 4.0

## Abstract

Computational developments—particularly artificial intelligence—are reshaping social scientific research and raise new questions for in-depth methods such as ethnography and qualitative interviewing. Building on classic debates about computers in qualitative data analysis (QDA), we revisit possibilities and dangers in an era of automation, Large Language Model (LLM) chatbots, and 'big data.' We introduce a typology of contemporary approaches to using computers in qualitative research: streamlining workflows, scaling up projects, hybrid analytical methods, the sociology of computation, and technological rejection. Drawing from scaled team ethnographies and solo research integrating computational social science (CSS), we describe methodological choices across study lifecycles, from literature reviews through data collection, coding, text retrieval, and representation. We argue that new technologies hold potential to address longstanding methodological challenges when deployed with knowledge, purpose, and ethical commitment. Yet a pragmatic approach—moving beyond technological optimism and dismissal—is essential given rapidly changing tools that are both generative and dangerous. Computation now saturates research infrastructure, from algorithmic literature searches to scholarly metrics, making computational literacy a core methodological competence in and beyond sociology. We conclude that when used carefully and transparently, contemporary computational tools can meaningfully expand—rather than displace—the irreducible insights of qualitative research.

**Keywords:** qualitative research, qualitative data analysis (QDA), computational social science (CSS), computational ethnography, mixed methods, artificial intelligence (AI), large language models (LLMs), open science, computational grounded theory, natural language processing (NLP), machine learning (ML)

## Key Sections Summary

### 1. Introduction: The Shifting Technological Landscape
- Computation now saturates research infrastructure (algorithmic literature searches, scholarly metrics)
- Need to move beyond dualism of "technological optimism vs. rejection"
- Computational literacy is becoming a core methodological competence

### 2. Qualitative Analysis and Computing: Debates Longstanding and New
- History of CAQDAS (Computer-Assisted Qualitative Data Analysis Software) from early tools
- Legacy from C. Wright Mills (filing cabinet as systematic engine) and W.E.B. Du Bois (data visualization)
- Longstanding concerns: imposition of quantitative logics, distancing from data, loss of contextual granularity
- New concerns: AI hallucinations, algorithmic biases

### 3. Five Approaches to Computation in Qualitative Research (THE TYPOLOGY)
1. **Streamlining/augmenting workflow** — making established practices more efficient (transcription, coding, retrieval)
2. **Scaling up qualitative projects** — using computation to handle larger datasets (e.g., American Voices Project)
3. **Hybrid analytical approaches** — mixed-methods integrating computational and interpretive analysis (e.g., computational grounded theory, ethnoarrays)
4. **Sociology of computation** — treating computational systems themselves as objects of sociological study
5. **Technological rejection** — principled avoidance based on epistemological or ethical grounds

### 4. Practical Workflows Across the Research Lifecycle
- Literature review: algorithmic search tools
- Data collection: transcription, OCR, satellite imagery
- Coding/indexing: ML-aided code scaling, semantic networks
- Text retrieval: systematic search across large corpora
- Representation: heatmaps, network visualizations, ethnoarrays

### 5. Key Arguments
- Coding should be reframed as "indexing" — systematic tagging for retrieval, not reductive categorization
- Computation can expand rather than displace the irreducible insights of qualitative research
- Tools are "both dangerous and generative, constraining and useful"
- Need purposeful deployment with knowledge and ethical commitment
