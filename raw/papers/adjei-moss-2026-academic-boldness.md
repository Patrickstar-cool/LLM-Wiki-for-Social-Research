# Academic Boldness: A Critical Resource for Decolonizing Knowledge

**Authors:** Stephen Baffour Adjei (University of Oslo) & Sigrun Marie Moss (BI Norwegian Business School)
**Published:** Human Arenas (Springer), 21 May 2026
**DOI:** 10.1007/s42087-026-00630-1
**Access:** 566

## Core Concept

**Academic boldness** = the willingness and confidence to be innovative and articulate one's unique knowledges, theories and concepts even if this may lead to pushback, questioning or being ignored. It is proposed as a critical resource for decolonizing knowledge.

## Relationship to Existing Concepts

- **Academic freedom**: Freedom to express views ≠ actually doing so boldly
- **Epistemic disobedience** (Mignolo): Delinking from Western modernity, but doesn't necessarily include articulating alternatives
- **Intellectual courage** (Baehr, Fleisher): Individual virtue of overcoming fear of not knowing
- **Epistemic agency** (Adjei et al.): Capacity to theorize from one's cultural experience — but agency without the courage to articulate is insufficient

## Historical Context

The paper traces the origins of epistemic exclusion to colonization. Colonial education produced "subordination, exploitation, mental confusion, and underdevelopment" (Rodney, 1972). Key mechanisms:
- **Epistemic unfreedom** (Ratele, 2024): Metaphorical entrapment in Western knowledge frameworks
- **Epistemic self-colonization**: Internalized inferiority causing scholars to perpetuate colonial mentality
- **Gatekeeping powers**: Western scholars/institutions determine what counts as legitimate knowledge

## African Examples of Academic Boldness

- **Negotiated agency / social intentionality** (Adjei, 2017-2019): Agency as negotiated within relational networks, not autonomous self-creation
- **Spiritualist model of psychopathology** (Nwoye, 2015): Spiritual origins of mental illness
- **Biopsychotheosocial model** (Oppong, 2017): Biological + psychological + theological + social dimensions
- Indigenous concepts: *Ubuntu*, *Omoluabi*, *Birget*

## Tensions

The colonial publishing infrastructure creates a dilemma: speaking boldly can be career-limiting, but silence perpetuates Western epistemic dominance. Academic boldness is not opposed to intellectual humility — one can be committed to one's beliefs while acknowledging limits.
