# Empathy in Chinese Culture: The Role of Differential Mode of Association

**Authors:** ZHU Xu, YANG Xue (Central China Normal University)

**Publication:** Advances in Psychological Science (心理科学进展), 2026, Vol. 34, Issue 6, pp. 1097-1108

**DOI:** 10.3724/SP.J.1042.2026.1097

## Abstract

Empathy, the capacity to share and understand the emotions and thoughts of others, is profoundly shaped by cultural context. However, prevailing cross-cultural research has largely been confined to dichotomous comparisons between individualism and collectivism, leaving systematic investigations into the mechanisms within specific cultural contexts underexplored. In contrast, Fei Xiaotong's indigenous sociological construct, termed the "differential mode of association" (chaxu geju), offers a more precise lens for understanding the structure of social networks and the ethical norms governing interpersonal interactions in China. This study adopts this framework to examine empathy in the Chinese cultural context.

## Key Findings

### The Two-Dimensional Framework of Chaxu Geju
- **Intimacy-distance (亲疏, qin-shu):** The gradient of closeness in Chinese social relations
- **Hierarchy-status (尊卑, zun-bei):** The status order within social relationships

### Dual Challenges to Western Empathy Models in Chinese Context

**1. Intimacy-distance dimension:**
- Chinese social relations tend toward self-other fusion, which conflicts with the clear self-other boundary presupposed by Western empathy models
- This can lead individuals to become overly absorbed in empathic engagement → heightened personal distress
- Such distress may paradoxically motivate avoidance of direct emotional empathy (self-protection)

**2. Hierarchy-status dimension:**
- Confucian emphasis on status order and role-based moral obligations constrains egalitarian empathy
- Empathizing beyond one's prescribed role identity may be perceived as inappropriate or offensive

### Three Characteristics of Chinese Empathy Mode

1. **Implicit and indirect emotional expression:**
   - Restraint of emotional disclosure with strangers
   - Even in close relationships, understanding conveyed through rationalization or action-based assistance rather than direct emotional attunement

2. **Holistic and rationalized cognition:**
   - Empathy proceeds from appraisal of overall relational and situational context
   - Guided by dialectical and compromise-oriented modes of thinking

3. **Problem-solving and action orientation:**
   - Due to blurred self-other boundaries, individuals assume greater responsibility for others
   - "Action speaks louder than words" — empathy expected to be realized through behavioral practices

### Theoretical Conclusion
Empathy should be understood as a **situated practice** shaped by specific social relations and ethical orders, not as a purely intrapsychic capacity. The Confucian ethical framework takes *ren* (仁, benevolence/humaneness) as internal emotional foundation and *li* (礼, ritual propriety) as external normative framework, giving rise to a culturally distinctive empathy mode.

**Keywords:** empathy, Chinese culture, differential mode of association, intimacy-distance, hierarchy-status
